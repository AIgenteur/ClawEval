## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

The steam from the miso broth curled off the ceramic bowl, carrying the scent of fermented soy and damp earth. Elias watched the liquid ripple as his chopsticks hovered, trembling slightly before he finally speared a slice of pork. Across the table, Mara didn't look up. She was dissecting a piece of seaweed with surgical precision, her movements rigid, controlled.

"Did you get the invitation for Leo's?" she asked, her voice flat.

Elias swallowed the pork, the salt burning his throat. "I did. The one for the surprise?"

"Right. The surprise." She finally met his eyes, her gaze sharp as a shard of glass. "I heard the venue is a bit... exposed. No walls, just a glass box in the middle of the park."

"Open concept," Elias corrected, tapping his spoon against the rim of his bowl. "Good for visibility. Hard to hide anything once the lights go on."

Mara smiled, a tight, brittle thing that didn't reach her eyes. "Exactly. Transparency is key. You can't have secrets in a glass box. Everyone sees everything."

"Nothing to hide," Elias said, leaning back. The leather of his chair creaked. "Just a celebration. A clean slate."

"Clean," she repeated, testing the word like it was a foreign currency. "I hope the budget reflects that. I heard the catering costs are astronomical this year. Some things are hard to balance."

Elias felt the heat rise in his cheeks, a flush that had nothing to do with the spicy broth. "I've been careful with the numbers. The books are... very clear. No discrepancies."

"Good," Mara said, finally tearing the seaweed in half. "Because a discrepancy can ruin a whole meal. Make you lose your appetite."

The waiter arrived, sliding a plate of dumplings between them. The steam rose in a thick, white cloud, momentarily obscuring Mara's face. She inhaled deeply, her eyes fluttering shut for a second. "Smells like home," she murmured. "Or maybe just like the past. Hard to tell the difference sometimes."

"I'm thinking of doing the cake myself," Elias said, his voice dropping an octave. "No decorators. Just me and the flour. Simple ingredients. No fillers."

"Simple is best," she agreed, picking up her chopsticks again. "Complex recipes often fall apart. Especially when the oven gets too hot."

The air in the restaurant felt heavy, thick with the smell of garlic and the low hum of a dozen other conversations that sounded like static. Elias watched her hands. They were steady, but her knuckles were white where she gripped the wood.

"Leo's going to love it," Elias said. "He always liked the straightforward stuff."

"He did," Mara said, a flicker of something dark passing behind her irises. "He liked things that were exactly what they said they were. No tricks. No hidden compartments."

"Exactly," Elias said, the word feeling like a stone in his mouth. "No tricks."

"Good," Mara said, finally taking a bite of a dumpling. She chewed slowly, her eyes never leaving his. "Because I hate it when people try to hide the filling. It's just a waste of good dough."

Elias nodded, the silence stretching between them, taut as a wire. The clinking of silverware sounded like gunshots in the quiet space.

"Pass the soy sauce?" he asked.

Mara slid the bottle across the table. It stopped just short of his hand. "Here. Don't spill it. It's hard to clean up a mess like that."

"I won't," Elias said, reaching for it. His fingers brushed the cold glass. "I'm very careful."

"Good," she whispered, turning back to her bowl. "Because we're all watching."
