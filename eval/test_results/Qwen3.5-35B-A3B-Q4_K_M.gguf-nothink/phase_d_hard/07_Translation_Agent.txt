### Spanish Translation

"Nuestro nuevo alojamiento Cloud9 pone tu sitio web en la **nube de los novios**! Como buen vecino, ServerFarm está ahí. No pongas todos tus huevos en una canasta: nuestra conmutación por error multi-región significa que tus datos están siempre seguros. Con velocidades que harían **celoso a Usain Bolt**, tus páginas cargan antes de que puedas decir 'supercalifragilisticexpialidocious'."

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: PUN "Cloud9/cloud nine"]**
In Spanish, the idiom "estar en la nube de los novios" (literally "in the cloud of the newlyweds") is a common expression for being in a state of bliss or extreme happiness, similar to "on cloud nine." However, since "Cloud9" is a specific brand name, I kept "Cloud9" as is for brand recognition but added the phrase "nube de los novios" immediately after to preserve the pun's meaning of being on top of the world. Alternatively, a more direct translation "en la nube nueve" would lose the idiomatic weight, so this hybrid approach ensures the user understands the emotional benefit while recognizing the product name.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: CULTURE-SPECIFIC REFERENCE "Like a good neighbor"]**
This is a direct reference to the famous State Farm insurance slogan ("Like a good neighbor, you are there"). In Spanish, this specific cultural touchstone does not exist. I translated it literally ("Como buen vecino, ServerFarm está ahí") to maintain the friendly, reassuring tone of the original copy. While Spanish speakers won't immediately recognize the State Farm reference, the phrase "buen vecino" is a universally positive concept in Hispanic cultures, effectively conveying the intended message of reliability and community support without needing the specific brand association.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: IDIOM "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"]**
This is a universal idiom. In Spanish, the equivalent is "No pongas todos tus huevos en una canasta." I used the direct translation as it is the standard, widely understood idiom in all Spanish-speaking regions, perfectly mirroring the English structure and meaning regarding risk management.

### Japanese Translation

"私たちの新しい Cloud9 ホスティングで、あなたのウェブサイトは**雲の上の九番街**に！良い隣人として、ServerFarm はいつもそこにいます。卵を一つのカゴに詰めるな — 私たちのマルチリージョンフェイルオーバーにより、あなたのデータは常に安全です。ウサイン・ボルトが**嫉妬する**ほどの速度で、あなたのページは『スーパーカリフラジリスティックエクスピアリドーシャス』と言う前に読み込まれます。"

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: PUN "Cloud9/cloud nine"]**
The English pun relies on the idiom "on cloud nine" (meaning extreme happiness) and the brand name "Cloud9." In Japanese, there is no direct equivalent idiom involving the number nine and clouds that conveys the same meaning. I translated the brand name "Cloud9" as is. For the idiom, I used "雲の上の九番街" (Kumo no ue no kyū-bangai), which literally means "Ninth Street above the clouds." This is a creative adaptation that attempts to preserve the "Cloud" and "Nine" elements visually, though it loses the specific idiom of "happiness." To make it work, the context implies a "heavenly" or "top-tier" location. A more literal translation of the idiom ("九番目の雲の上") would sound unnatural, so this adaptation prioritizes the brand name while hinting at the "high" status.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: CULTURE-SPECIFIC REFERENCE "Like a good neighbor"]**
The "Like a good neighbor" slogan is deeply rooted in US culture (State Farm) and has no equivalent in Japan. I translated it as "良い隣人として" (Yoi rinjin toshite), meaning "As a good neighbor." While Japanese audiences won't recognize the specific insurance slogan, the concept of a "good neighbor" is culturally positive and implies trustworthiness and reliability in Japanese business contexts. I did not attempt to substitute it with a Japanese brand slogan, as that would be misleading; instead, I focused on the literal meaning to maintain the tone of service and proximity.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: IDIOM "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"]**
The Japanese equivalent of this idiom is "卵を一つのカゴに詰めるな" (Tamago o hitotsu no kago ni tsumeru na). This is a direct translation that is fully understood in Japan, often used in business and financial contexts to advise against concentrating risk. It fits perfectly with the technical context of "failover" and data safety.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: POP CULTURE REFERENCE "Usain Bolt"]**
Usain Bolt is a globally recognized figure (the fastest man alive), so his name is transliterated as ウサイン・ボルト (Usain Bōruto). The concept of "speed" is universal, so no adaptation was needed for the reference itself, though the phrasing "make jealous" (嫉妬する) is used to convey the intensity of the comparison.

**[TRANSLATOR NOTE: POP CULTURE REFERENCE "Supercalifragilisticexpialidocious"]**
This word from *Mary Poppins* is a proper noun and is treated as a loanword in Japanese. I used the standard Katakana transliteration "スーパーカリフラジリスティックエクスピアリドーシャス" to ensure the audience recognizes the specific reference to the song, even if they don't know the meaning of the word.
