### [TRANSLATOR NOTES]  

**1. Pun: "Cloud9/cloud nine"**  
- **Spanish:** Preserved "Cloud9" as a brand name and translated "cloud nine" as "nube nueve," which is a direct play on the idiom "en la nube nueve" (literally "on cloud nine"). While the phrase "nube nueve" is less common in Spanish, it retains the pun's double meaning.  
- **Japanese:** Kept "Cloud9" as a brand name and used "雲の上" (kumo no ue), which literally translates to "on the cloud." The pun is preserved through the brand name, but the idiom "cloud nine" is not a standard Japanese expression, so the translation prioritizes clarity over literalism.  

**2. Culture-specific reference: "Like a good neighbor, ServerFarm is there"**  
- **Spanish:** Retained the phrase "Como un buen vecino" (like a good neighbor) but added a note explaining that it references the State Farm insurance slogan. In Spanish-speaking markets, this reference may not be immediately recognizable, so the translation assumes the audience is familiar with the brand.  
- **Japanese:** Translated as "良い隣人のように" (like a good neighbor), but the note clarifies that this is a direct reference to the State Farm slogan. In Japan, the slogan is not widely known, so the translation adapts it to maintain the intended tone without assuming prior knowledge.  

**3. Idiom: "Don't put all your eggs in one basket"**  
- **Spanish:** Used the direct translation "No metas todos tus huevos en una canasta," which is a common equivalent in Spanish.  
- **Japanese:** Translated as "一つの籠にすべての卵を入れないでください" (ichizutsu no kago ni subete no tamago o irenaide kudasai), which is a direct equivalent. The idiom is preserved, though it is less commonly used in Japanese than in English.
