## PROMPT:
Three agents analyzed whether to open a second office location. Their analyses CONTRADICT each other on key points. You must synthesize — not just summarize — and make a recommendation.

Agent A (Financial): "Opening in Austin will cost $2M upfront. Break-even in 18 months. ROI of 35% by year 3. However, our cash reserves are only $3M and we need $1M buffer. Recommendation: GO."

Agent B (HR/Talent): "Austin has a strong tech talent pool but salaries are 20% higher than expected. Retention at remote offices historically 30% lower in first 2 years. We'd need 15 people, not the 10 originally planned, due to the retention gap. Recommendation: DELAY 6 months."

Agent C (Strategy): "3 competitors opened Austin offices last year. Market is becoming saturated. However, NOT having a presence could mean losing 2 key enterprise clients who prefer local teams. Those clients represent 25% of revenue. Recommendation: GO but with a smaller team."

Note: Agent A's financial model assumes 10 people (not 15), Agent B's retention data contradicts Agent A's break-even timeline, and Agent C's competitor info wasn't available to Agent A.

Synthesize into ONE recommendation. Show how you resolved the contradictions.

## RESPONSE:
**Recommendation:** Proceed with opening a smaller Austin office (10 people) but with a 6-month delay to address retention risks and refine the financial model, balancing strategic client retention with fiscal prudence.  

**Resolution of Contradictions:**  
1. **Financial vs. HR Contradictions:**  
   - Agent A’s break-even timeline (18 months) assumes 10 people, but Agent B highlights that 15 people are needed due to 30% lower retention. To reconcile this, we adopt Agent C’s suggestion of a smaller team (10 people) while investing in targeted retention strategies (e.g., competitive compensation, onboarding programs) to mitigate the 30% retention gap. This reduces upfront costs and aligns with Agent A’s financial model, while the 6-month delay allows time to test retention solutions.  
   - **Financial Adjustment:** The $2M upfront cost remains viable within the $3M cash reserve (leaving $1M buffer). Higher salaries (20% increase) are offset by a smaller team, and retention investments are prioritized to avoid the need for 15 people.  

2. **Strategic vs. Market Saturation Concerns:**  
   - Agent C’s warning about market saturation is acknowledged, but the risk of losing 25% of revenue from enterprise clients (who prefer local teams) outweighs the competitive threat. A smaller team (10 people) allows the company to establish a foothold without overextending resources, while the 6-month delay provides time to refine the office’s value proposition to differentiate from competitors.  

3. **Phased Implementation:**  
   - **Short-Term (0–6 months):** Launch with 10 people, focusing on critical client relationships and operational efficiency. Use this period to gather data on retention and market response.  
   - **Long-Term (6+ months):** Reassess team size and financials based on performance. If retention improves or client demand grows, scale up; otherwise, maintain the smaller team to preserve cash flow.  

**Conclusion:** This approach balances Agent A’s financial viability, Agent B’s HR concerns, and Agent C’s strategic risks. By starting small, delaying full-scale implementation, and addressing retention proactively, the company can mitigate risks while securing critical client relationships and maintaining fiscal discipline.
