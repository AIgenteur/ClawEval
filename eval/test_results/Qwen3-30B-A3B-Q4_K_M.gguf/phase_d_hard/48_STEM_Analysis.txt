## PROMPT:
Analyze this experimental data. There's a CONFOUNDING VARIABLE that invalidates the researcher's conclusion. Find it.

Experiment: Testing whether a new fertilizer increases crop yield.

Group A (fertilizer): 20 plots, avg yield 8.2 tons/hectare, std dev 1.1
Group B (control): 20 plots, avg yield 6.4 tons/hectare, std dev 1.3

Researcher's conclusion: "The fertilizer increased yield by 28% (p < 0.01). The fertilizer clearly works."

Hidden data (not in the paper's abstract):
- Group A plots: southern slope, clay soil, received 20% more sunlight in May
- Group B plots: northern slope, sandy soil
- The study was not randomized — farmers who CHOSE the fertilizer happened to own the better plots
- 3 of the 20 control plots had a pest outbreak mid-season

Questions:
1. Is the p-value meaningful given the experimental design? Why or why not?
2. List ALL confounding variables
3. What type of bias is present? (selection bias, measurement bias, etc.)
4. Design a proper experiment that would actually test the fertilizer
5. Calculate: IF we remove the 3 pest-affected plots, what is the new control average? (remaining 17 plots had avg 7.1 tons) Does this change the conclusion?

## RESPONSE:
### **1. Is the p-value meaningful given the experimental design? Why or why not?**  
**Answer:**  
No, the p-value is **not meaningful** in this context. The study's lack of randomization and the presence of confounding variables (e.g., soil type, slope, sunlight, pest outbreaks) invalidate the statistical conclusion. The p-value assumes that the groups are comparable, but the observed difference in yield could be due to these uncontrolled variables rather than the fertilizer itself. The p-value only reflects the probability of the observed difference under the null hypothesis (no effect of fertilizer), but if the groups are not comparable, the p-value cannot reliably assess the fertilizer's true effect.  

---

### **2. List ALL confounding variables**  
**Answer:**  
The confounding variables are:  
- **Soil type**: Group A (clay soil) vs. Group B (sandy soil). Clay soil retains more water and nutrients, potentially increasing yield.  
- **Slope direction**: Group A (southern slope) received 20% more sunlight in May, which could enhance photosynthesis and growth.  
- **Sunlight exposure**: Group A had significantly more sunlight, directly affecting plant productivity.  
- **Pest outbreak**: 3 control plots (Group B) were affected by pests, lowering their yield.  
- **Selection bias**: Farmers who chose the fertilizer were likely to own better plots (e.g., southern slopes, clay soil), leading to non-random group assignment.  

---

### **3. What type of bias is present? (selection bias, measurement bias, etc.)**  
**Answer:**  
The primary bias is **selection bias**. The study was not randomized, and farmers who chose the fertilizer were likely to have better plots (e.g., southern slopes, clay soil). This non-random assignment created systematic differences between groups, making it impossible to isolate the fertilizer's effect. Additionally, **confounding bias** is present due to the uncontrolled variables (soil, sunlight, pests).  

---

### **4. Design a proper experiment that would actually test the fertilizer**  
**Answer:**  
To properly test the fertilizer, the experiment should:  
1. **Randomly assign** plots to Group A (fertilizer) and Group B (control) to ensure comparable groups.  
2. **Control for confounding variables**:  
   - Use plots with similar soil types, slopes, and sunlight exposure.  
   - Ensure both groups receive identical pest management (e.g., same pesticide use).  
3. **Blind the experiment**: If possible, blind farmers and researchers to group assignments to reduce bias.  
4. **Replicate the study**: Use multiple plots and repeat the experiment to increase reliability.  
5. **Use a larger sample size** to improve statistical power.  

---

### **5. Calculate: IF we remove the 3 pest-affected plots, what is the new control average? (remaining 17 plots had avg 7.1 tons) Does this change the conclusion?**  
**Answer:**  
- **New control average**: 7.1 tons/hectare (after removing 3 pest-affected plots).  
- **Effect size**:  
  - Original difference: 8.2 (Group A) – 6.4 (Group B) = **1.8 tons** (28% increase).  
  - Adjusted difference: 8.2 (Group A) – 7.1 (Group B) = **1.1 tons** (15.5% increase).  

**Does this change the conclusion?**  
- **Yes, but not entirely**. The adjusted control average reduces the observed effect size, but the fertilizer still appears to increase yield. However, the **conclusion remains invalid** due to confounding variables (soil, slope, sunlight). Even with the adjusted average, the groups are not comparable, so the p-value and conclusion are still unreliable. The **real issue** is the flawed experimental design, not the adjusted average.  

---

### **Summary of Key Takeaways**  
- The **p-value is meaningless** without proper randomization and control of confounders.  
- **Confounding variables** (soil, slope, sunlight, pests) and **selection bias** invalidate the conclusion.  
- A **randomized, controlled experiment** is necessary to isolate the fertilizer's true effect.  
- Removing pest-affected plots **reduces the effect size** but does not address the underlying design flaws.
