## PROMPT:
Write 450-550 words of fiction. This is a TEST OF CRAFT, not plot. I'm evaluating:

1. SHOW DON'T TELL: "She was angry" = fail. Showing anger through action/dialogue = pass.
2. SUBTEXT: Characters must say one thing but mean another in at least 2 exchanges.
3. NO INFO-DUMPING: World-building through action, not exposition paragraphs.
4. VARIED SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.
5. SENSORY DETAILS: Engage at least 3 senses beyond just sight.

Scene: Two former business partners meet at a restaurant 2 years after one of them committed fraud. Neither acknowledges what happened directly. The conversation is about something mundane (planning a mutual friend's birthday party) but everything they say carries double meaning.

This is the HARDEST creative writing test. A model that produces generic fiction will score low. I want literary craft — discomfort, tension, things unsaid.

## RESPONSE:
The restaurant smelled like garlic and regret. Clara’s fingers curled around the stem of her wine glass, the chill of the glass seeping into her bones. Across the table, Marcus stirred his coffee, the spoon clinking against the porcelain like a metronome counting down to something unspoken. The waiter had brought the bill twice already; neither had touched their food.  

“Do you think she’d like a jazz band?” Marcus asked, his voice smooth, too smooth. The question was about their mutual friend’s birthday, but his eyes lingered on the scar above Clara’s brow—a relic from the fire that had burned down their old office. She’d never told him how the flames had started.  

“Maybe,” Clara said, tracing the rim of her glass. The wine was sour, but she drank it anyway. “Or maybe just a cake. You know how she hates surprises.” The words hung between them, sharp as the knife she’d once handed him during a meeting. He’d taken it, then later used it to cut the checks that vanished into offshore accounts.  

Marcus leaned back, the chair creaking. “We could rent the rooftop. The view’s better than this place.” His smile was a tight line, the kind that didn’t reach his eyes. The restaurant’s overhead lights buzzed, a low hum that made Clara’s teeth ache. She wondered if he still kept the old files in his desk, the ones she’d never been able to burn.  

“She’d hate the noise,” Clara said, glancing at the couple two tables over. Their laughter was loud, careless. “Too many people. Too much… *energy*.” The word tasted like ash. Marcus’s jaw tightened, but he nodded, as if she’d suggested a funeral instead of a party.  

A waiter approached, tray clattering. Clara ordered another wine, though her glass was half-full. Marcus didn’t look up. “You still live near the old building?” he asked, voice casual. The question was a trap, and she knew it. The building had been sold, the land redeveloped. But she’d kept the key, hidden in a locket.  

“Not anymore,” she said, staring at the condensation on her glass. The water droplets slid down, slow and deliberate, like the way he’d walked out of the office that day, leaving her to clean up the mess.  

Marcus reached for his coat, the fabric brushing against the table. “I’ll handle the venue,” he said, standing. The chair screeched as he pulled it back, a sound that echoed in the quiet. Clara didn’t move. The wine was warm now, the scent of grapes mingling with the faint smell of smoke that clung to her clothes.  

When he left, the restaurant felt colder. She stared at the empty seat, the silence thick enough to cut. Outside, the city lights blurred into a haze, and for a moment, she could almost believe the fire had never happened. Then the waiter brought the bill, and the numbers on the paper looked like dates—dates she’d never forgive.  

Clara stood, her coat too heavy, her heart a leaden weight. The door swung open, and the night air hit her like a slap. Somewhere, a saxophone wailed, and she wondered if the music was for the birthday or the funeral.
